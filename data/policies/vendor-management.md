## 1. Purpose

This policy establishes how Meridian Trust Bank, N.A. ("the Bank") identifies, assesses, manages, and monitors the risks of its third-party relationships throughout the life cycle, consistent with the Interagency Guidance on Third-Party Relationships: Risk Management (FRB SR 23-4 / FDIC FIL-29-2023 / OCC Bulletin 2023-17, June 6, 2023, the "2023 Interagency Guidance"), the Bank Service Company Act (12 U.S.C. 1867(c)), Section 501(b) of the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (15 U.S.C. 6801), the Interagency Guidelines Establishing Information Security Standards (12 C.F.R. Part 30 Appendix B), and applicable consumer protection laws including UDAAP (12 U.S.C. 5531), Regulation E (12 C.F.R. Part 1005), and Regulation P (12 C.F.R. Part 1016).

Vendor concentration, performance failures, control gaps, SOC 2 exceptions, vendor security incidents, and vendor consumer complaints feed the operational risk program, the information security program (POL-IS-001), the consumer complaint program (POL-COMP-001), the fair lending program (POL-FL-001), and the BSA/AML program (POL-BSA-001).

The Bank's use of a third party does not diminish its responsibility to perform activities in a safe and sound manner and in compliance with applicable laws. The same compliance and risk-management standards that apply to in-house activities apply to third-party activities performed on the Bank's behalf.

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to every business arrangement between the Bank and another entity, by contract or otherwise, regardless of whether the entity is bank or non-bank, affiliated or unaffiliated, regulated or unregulated, domestic or foreign. Covered arrangements include outsourced services, technology services, joint ventures, subcontracting and "fourth-party" relationships reached through a direct third party, referral and lead-generation relationships, marketing and co-branding arrangements, payment system providers, cloud-services providers, model and data providers, and merchant or affiliate programs.

The policy applies to every employee, officer, director, business unit, and committee that initiates, negotiates, approves, manages, monitors, or terminates a third-party relationship on the Bank's behalf. Out of scope are internal functions performed by Bank employees, ordinary-course retail purchases of commodity goods that do not give a counterparty access to confidential customer information or to a Bank system, and one-time professional engagements below the de minimis threshold set by the TPRMO.

This policy supersedes any prior vendor management policy. Where this policy conflicts with applicable law, regulation, or supervisory guidance, the law or guidance controls.

## 3. Definitions

**Third-Party Relationship.** Any business arrangement between the Bank and another entity, by contract or otherwise, as defined by the 2023 Interagency Guidance. The label (vendor, supplier, partner, contractor, processor) does not control; what controls is whether the third party performs an activity on the Bank's behalf or has access to confidential Bank or customer information or systems.

**Critical Activity.** A relationship that involves significant Bank functions (e.g., payments, clearing, settlements, custody) or significant shared services (e.g., information technology, core processing); or a relationship that could cause significant risk to the Bank if the third party fails to meet expectations; or a relationship with significant customer impact; or a relationship requiring significant investment to implement and manage. Critical activities require enhanced oversight and Board-level reporting at least annually.

**Subcontractor / Fourth Party.** Any entity engaged by a Bank third party to perform any portion of an activity provided to the Bank. The Bank's risk-management obligations extend to the third party's use of subcontractors; subcontracting does not transfer the Bank's compliance or oversight obligations.

**Concentration Risk.** The risk that the failure or material disruption of a single third party, a small set of third parties performing related activities, or a geography or platform on which multiple third parties depend would cause significant risk to the Bank because of volume, dollar amount, strategic importance, or limited availability of alternatives.

**Service-Level Agreement (SLA).** A contractually binding measurable commitment to deliver an activity at a specified performance level (uptime, latency, throughput, error rate, response time, resolution time), with defined remedies for breach (service credits, root-cause analysis, escalation rights, termination rights).

**Bank Service Company Act ("BSCA") Notification.** The notice the Bank is required to file with its primary federal regulator under 12 U.S.C. 1867(c)(2) for the performance of bank services by a third party, including services performed by an affiliate or non-bank service provider. Required at the start of the relationship and on material change.

**Right to Audit.** The contractually reserved right of the Bank, its auditors, and its primary federal regulator to examine the third party's books, records, controls, and operations relevant to the services, on reasonable notice, throughout the term and for a defined period after termination.

**Exit / Termination Plan.** A documented, tested plan for orderly transition of the activity from the third party back to the Bank or to a successor in the event of expected or unexpected termination, including timelines, responsibilities, customer-impact mitigation, data return and destruction, and continuity of regulatory and customer obligations during transition.

## 4. Governance, Roles, and Responsibilities

The Board of Directors is ultimately responsible for oversight of third-party relationships. The Board approves this policy at least annually, approves the Bank's third-party risk appetite, receives at least annual reporting on critical-activity third parties, and approves the response to any material third-party incident. Senior management implements the policy, ensures adequate resources, assigns each relationship to a named accountable Business Owner, and reports operating effectiveness to the Board.

The Third-Party Risk Management Office (TPRMO), led by the Chief Risk Officer, owns this policy and the enterprise third-party inventory, sets the risk-rating methodology and tiering thresholds, sets minimum due-diligence and monitoring standards by tier, approves all proposed new and terminated critical-activity relationships before signature or notice, performs concentration-risk monitoring, and escalates unresolved findings.

Each Business Owner is the first line for the relationships in their line of business: identifies and proposes them, conducts the risk assessment, executes due diligence with TPRMO support, negotiates the contract with Legal, performs ongoing monitoring, escalates issues, and executes termination and transition plans. Compliance, Information Security, IT, Privacy, BSA/AML, Legal, Internal Audit, and Finance act as second-line subject-matter reviewers. Internal Audit independently tests the program at a frequency commensurate with risk and at least every two years.

## 5. Risk Assessment and Tiering

Before entering any relationship, the Business Owner, with TPRMO oversight, performs a risk assessment considering, at a minimum: strategic purpose; nature and volume of the activity; whether the activity is critical; whether the third party will have access to confidential Bank or customer information (including NPI under GLBA and Regulation P), to a Bank system, or to Bank customers; whether the activity is subject to consumer protection laws (Regulation E, Regulation Z, Regulation B, FHA, UDAAP, FCRA, FDCPA), BSA/AML, or OFAC; geographic location, including services performed outside the United States; reputational considerations; and availability of alternatives.

The TPRMO then assigns one of three tiers. **Tier 1 (Critical).** Critical activities under Section 3, or any relationship the TPRMO designates as critical. Requires enhanced due diligence; enhanced contract terms (right to audit, SLA-backed performance, defined termination rights, examination cooperation); enhanced ongoing monitoring (annual SOC 2 Type II review, annual financial review, quarterly KPI/SLA review, complaint trend review); annual Board reporting; and a documented, periodically tested exit plan. **Tier 2 (Significant).** Relationships involving access to confidential customer information, a Bank system, customer-facing activity, or consumer-protection-regulated activity but not critical. Requires risk-based due diligence, appropriate contract terms, at least annual monitoring, and a documented exit approach. **Tier 3 (Limited).** Relationships without access to confidential customer information, a Bank system, or Bank customers, and not consumer-protection-regulated. Requires baseline due diligence (financial standing, OFAC screening, conflicts review), standard contract terms, and exception-based monitoring.

Tiering is not a mechanism to avoid required compliance. Where a rule (GLBA, BSA, the 2023 Interagency Guidance for critical activities) prescribes a specific control, the control applies regardless of tier.

## 6. Due Diligence and Selection

For every proposed new relationship, and on the cadence in Section 9 for existing ones, the Business Owner conducts due diligence sufficient for the tier and proportionate to the risk. Due diligence covers, at a minimum: strategies and goals; legal and regulatory history (licenses, consent orders, fines, settlements, material litigation in the prior five years); financial condition (audited statements for the prior three fiscal years, key ratios, distress indicators; for Tier 1, an independent annual review); business experience and qualifications; operational and technology capacity at the Bank's scale; and risk management and controls (SOC 1 / SOC 2 Type II reports for the two most recent periods, ISO 27001 equivalent where relevant, user-control-considerations, and the third party's framework for fourth parties).

**Information security and privacy.** Compliance with the Interagency Guidelines Establishing Information Security Standards (12 C.F.R. Part 30 Appendix B); encryption at-rest and in-transit, access management, logging, vulnerability management, prior-12-months penetration-test results; GLBA / Regulation P compliance; data-flow mapping. **Business continuity and incident response.** BC/DR plan with RTO/RPO commitments and recent test results; incident history; breach-notification commitments meeting or exceeding the Computer-Security Incident Notification Rule (12 C.F.R. Part 53). **Complaint handling.** For consumer-facing third parties, intake and routing, volumes and trends, and ability to deliver complaint data for POL-COMP-001. **Subcontracting, insurance, conflicts of interest, OFAC, and reputation.** Process for selecting and overseeing subcontractors and flow-down of Bank-required terms; insurance types (general liability, E&O, cyber, fidelity) and limits with the Bank as additional insured or loss payee where appropriate; existing or potential conflicts including ownership and Bank-insider affiliations; OFAC and sanctions screening of the third party, principals, and known beneficial owners at onboarding and annually; negative news, enforcement history, civil litigation, and media coverage.

For Tier 1, findings, gaps, and proposed mitigations are documented in a due-diligence package reviewed and approved by the TPRMO before contract signature. Material gaps not mitigable through contract terms or compensating controls require senior-management approval and are reported to the Board.

## 7. Contract Structuring and Negotiation

Every third-party relationship is governed by a written contract, executed by an authorized signatory, before performance begins. Contracts are drafted and negotiated by Legal with the Business Owner, the TPRMO, and subject-matter reviewers. The following provisions are required for Tier 1 and Tier 2 contracts and included in Tier 3 to the extent appropriate to the risk.

**Scope, SLAs, and information.** Clear description of activities, services, locations, and duration. Specific measurable performance commitments (uptime, latency, response time, accuracy, error rates, complaint resolution time, security event response time) with measurement methodology, reporting cadence, and remedies for breach (service credits, root-cause analysis, escalation, Bank termination). Allocation of record creation, retention, and production for examinations and litigation holds; data return and destruction certifications on termination.

**Right to audit and examination cooperation.** The Bank's and its auditors' right to audit the third party's books, records, controls, and operations relevant to the services. Express agreement to cooperate with examinations and supervisory directives of the Bank's primary federal regulator under 12 U.S.C. 1867(c) and the 2023 Interagency Guidance, including direct access to records, personnel, and premises. **Compliance with applicable laws.** Express agreement to comply with all applicable laws, including Regulation E, Regulation Z, Regulation B, FHA, UDAAP, FCRA, FDCPA, GLBA / Regulation P, BSA and OFAC sanctions, ECOA, SCRA, and TCPA where applicable. **Compensation.** Fee structure and billing cadence. Volume- or revenue-based compensation that could create UDAAP or fair lending steering risk is prohibited unless reviewed by Compliance and Fair Lending.

**Information security, ownership, and risk allocation.** Obligation to maintain confidentiality, security, integrity, and availability of confidential Bank and customer information consistent with the Interagency Guidelines Establishing Information Security Standards. Bank ownership of customer and Bank-supplied data; license terms for third-party IP. Third-party indemnification for losses arising from breach, negligence, willful misconduct, or violation of law. Required insurance (types, minimum limits, additional-insured / loss-payee status, notice of cancellation). Governing law, forum, and escalation procedures.

**Subcontracting and termination.** The third party must identify proposed material subcontractors in writing, obtain Bank consent, and flow down information-security, confidentiality, audit, breach-notification, and compliance terms. Termination for cause (SLA failure, material breach, regulatory action, change of control, financial distress), termination for convenience, transition assistance, and return or destruction of Bank and customer data on a defined schedule with written certification. **Customer complaints.** Routing to the Bank for customer-facing third parties, reporting cadence, and cooperation under POL-COMP-001. **Notification of material changes.** Advance notice of change of control, material adverse financial change, material change in subcontractors or geographic location of services, material security incident, or material adverse regulatory action. **BSCA notification.** Where the contract triggers a BSCA notification, terms support timely Bank notification under 12 U.S.C. 1867(c) and update on material change.

Form contracts that omit any required provision or reverse the allocation of risk must be redlined to comply. Material deviations require TPRMO and Legal approval; for Tier 1, senior-management approval and Board reporting are also required.

## 8. Bank Service Company Act Notification and Critical-Activity Reporting

The Bank files a BSCA notification with its primary federal regulator under 12 U.S.C. 1867(c)(2) for the performance of bank services by a third party, regardless of affiliation or whether the third party is itself a bank. The TPRMO, with Legal, maintains the inventory of BSCA-reportable services and ensures notifications are filed at the start of each reportable relationship and updated on material change. Missed notifications are reported to senior management and remediated promptly.

For each Tier 1 third party, the TPRMO maintains a current critical-activity profile (identity, activity, Business Owner, contract dates, current risk rating, last due-diligence / SOC / financial review dates, open issues, exit plan status). The portfolio is reported to the Board at least annually.

## 9. Ongoing Monitoring

After contract execution, the Business Owner, with TPRMO and subject-matter-reviewer support, conducts ongoing monitoring proportional to the tier.

**SLA and performance.** Each measurable SLA is tracked against the contractual commitment on at least the cadence specified in the contract (typically monthly for Tier 1, quarterly for Tier 2). Breaches trigger root-cause analysis, remediation, and contractual escalation; repeated or unremediated breaches escalate to TPRMO and senior management. **Financial condition.** For Tier 1, an independent review is performed at least annually. Material adverse changes (going-concern qualification, regulatory action, decline in key ratios) trigger an interim review and contingency reassessment. For Tier 2, financial review is performed on a risk-based cadence at least biennially.

**Independent control reviews.** For Tier 1, and for Tier 2 relationships involving access to confidential customer information or a Bank system, the most recent SOC 1 / SOC 2 Type II report (or ISO 27001 equivalent) is reviewed at least annually. Gaps, complementary user-entity-control responsibilities, and unresolved prior-period findings are evaluated by Information Security, Internal Audit, and the TPRMO. Material gaps trigger compensating controls, contractual remediation, or escalation. Material subcontractor changes require notification under Section 7 and trigger risk reassessment.

**Security events.** Third-party-originated security incidents, including those at subcontractors, are tracked. Incidents meeting the Computer-Security Incident Notification Rule (12 C.F.R. Part 53) thresholds are reported to the Bank's primary federal regulator within the 36-hour window. **Consumer complaints.** Complaints involving third-party activity are routed into POL-COMP-001 and trended; adverse trends attributable to a single third party trigger interim review and, where appropriate, remediation or termination. **Regulatory and reputation events.** Negative news, regulatory actions, consent orders, settlements, material litigation, and change-of-control events trigger reassessment. **Concentration risk.** The TPRMO monitors third-party concentration at the enterprise level at least annually — by activity, geography, underlying technology platform (e.g., cloud-services provider), and single third party across multiple activities. Concentrations exceeding risk appetite are reported to senior management with proposed mitigations. **Issue tracking.** Findings are logged in the issues register with severity, owner, target remediation date, and status, and reported to senior management at least quarterly and to the Board at least annually.

## 10. Termination, Transition, and Exit Planning

Every Tier 1 relationship has a documented exit / transition plan maintained by the Business Owner with TPRMO oversight. The plan addresses successor capability (in-house or alternative third party), transition timeline, resource and cost estimates, data return and destruction, customer-impact mitigation and required customer notices, continuity of regulatory and customer obligations during transition, and conditions triggering an unplanned exit. Tier 1 plans are tested or tabletop-exercised on a risk-based cadence and at least every two years. For Tier 2, a documented exit approach is maintained.

Termination, whether for cause, for convenience, or at expiration, follows the contract and the exit plan. Before notice of termination is given for a Tier 1 relationship, the TPRMO reviews and approves the termination, exit plan readiness, and customer-impact analysis. After termination, the third party's obligation to return or destroy Bank and customer data is tracked to certified completion, and post-termination audit and recordkeeping rights survive for the contractual period.

## 11. Recordkeeping

The TPRMO maintains the enterprise inventory of all third-party relationships, including status, tier, Business Owner, contract dates, last due-diligence date, last monitoring date, BSCA status, and open issues. Risk assessments, due-diligence packages, executed contracts and amendments, monitoring artifacts (SLA reports, SOC reviews, financial reviews, complaint trends), incident records, examination work product, exit plans, and termination records are retained for the life of the relationship and for at least seven years after termination, or longer where law or regulation requires.

## 12. Compliance Monitoring

The TPRMO and Compliance jointly monitor compliance through a continuous rule-set. Indicators include: relationships in production without an approved contract; Tier 1 relationships without a current due-diligence package, SOC review, financial review, or exit plan; missed BSCA notifications; expired contracts with continuing performance; SLA breaches without root-cause closure; aged unresolved issues; concentration metrics exceeding risk appetite; unreported security incidents past the 12 C.F.R. Part 53 36-hour window; and any third-party-attributable consumer complaint trend. Deviations are tracked to closure, reported to senior management at least quarterly, and to the Board at least annually. Internal Audit independently tests this program at a frequency commensurate with risk and at least every two years.

## 13. Training

All personnel involved in initiating, negotiating, approving, managing, monitoring, or terminating third-party relationships receive annual training commensurate with their role. Business Owners of Tier 1 relationships receive enhanced annual training on the 2023 Interagency Guidance life cycle, contract requirements under Section 7, BSCA notification, incident response and breach notification, and exit planning. Non-completion bars further onboarding or contract execution by the affected personnel until remedied.

## 14. Violations and Enforcement

Violations of this policy are reported to the TPRMO and Compliance. Material violations — including unauthorized engagement of a third party, intentional avoidance of due diligence or contract requirements, or concealment of a third-party incident — are escalated to senior management and the Audit Committee, may result in disciplinary action up to termination of employment, and may be referred to law enforcement or regulators where required by law. Contracts that materially fail to meet this policy are subject to remediation or termination.
